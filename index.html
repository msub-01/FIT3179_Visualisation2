THE CHANGING FACE OF HIV AIDS

30 years ago a rare immuno-system virus was detected in 5 men in USA. By the following summer, the illness was spreading nationwide and had been given a name: Acquired Immuno Deficiency Syndrome, or AIDS.

In the following decades, AIDS has claimed millions of lives around the world. Thankfully, with proper education and advanced medicine, recent trends suggest a decline in AIDS-related death rates.

But we've still got a long way left to go.

Death rates are high across Sub-Saharan Africa

In the interactive map, we see the distribution of death rates worldwide. Most countries have a rate of less than 50 deaths per 100,000, often much lower, below 5 per 100,000. Across Europe, the death rate is less than one per 100,000. Across Sub-Saharan Africa, the rates are much higher. Most countries in the South of the region had rates greater than 100 per 100,000.


The 1990s saw a substantial increase in people infected with HIV and dying of the disease

Since then, the number of new infections began to decline, and in 2019, reached the lowest number since 1990. The graph also shows the continuing increase in the number of people living with HIV. The growth rate has slowed compared to the 1990s , but the absolute number is at the highest ever.



HIV AIDS is particulary deadly within younger demographics

Although the death count has declined with time, younger populations, particularly aged within 15 to 49 years are more susceptible to the virus.



Gender and Racial disparity in AIDS deaths in the United States



From the beginning, HIV AIDS has indeed been a "politically charged" disease. It has pitted rich against poor, and puritans against realists.

In most of the world, HIV AIDS originally affected fairly marginalised groups, usually sex workers, men who have sex with men and intravenous drug users. In most societies these people are very vulnerable.

When it comes to receiving help from taxpayers, these vulnerable groups are generally not at the top of the agenda.


In the United States, some racial/ethnic groups are more affected than others

This is because some population groups have higher rates of HIV in their communities, thus raising the risk of new infections with each sexual or injection drug use encounter.


More men tend to die from AIDS every year than women

The reasons for higher death rates in men include:

  • Starting treatment at later stages of disease progression

  • Worse adherance to treatment follow ups compared to female counterparts
